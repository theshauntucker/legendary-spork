# The CES Letter: Understanding the Most Influential Mormon Deconstruction Document

The CES Letter is a comprehensive document written by Jeremy Runnells that compiles historical, doctrinal, and logical problems with the LDS Church. Published online in 2013, it has become the most widely read critical document about Mormonism, credited with triggering the faith deconstruction of tens of thousands of members.

## What is the CES Letter?

CES stands for Church Educational System, the LDS Church's system for providing religious education. Runnells originally wrote the document as a personal letter to his local religious educator in the CES, requesting clarification on specific historical and doctrinal issues.

When the church didn't provide satisfactory answers, Runnells refined the document and published it online. What was intended as a personal letter became a comprehensive deconstruction tool used worldwide.

## The Letter's Format and Structure

The CES Letter is written as a sincere appeal from a believer to church leadership, requesting explanation for troubling historical and doctrinal problems. Rather than approaching the issues as "attacks," Runnells frames them as genuine questions a sincere member would have.

The document is approximately 70 pages and organized into specific sections, each addressing distinct categories of problems.

## Main Sections of the CES Letter

### The Translation and Historicity of the Book of Mormon

This section examines issues with the Book of Mormon's historicity:
- The absence of archaeological evidence for Book of Mormon civilizations
- Joseph Smith's use of the same "seer stone" method for translating the Book of Mormon as he used for folk magic treasure hunting
- Linguistic and structural analysis showing the Book of Mormon reflects 19th-century Protestant Christianity rather than ancient Mesoamerican cultures
- The evolution of the Book of Mormon translation narrative (from Joseph reading directly off the stone to using a hat)

### The Translation and Historicity of the Book of Abraham

The Book of Abraham exemplifies the translation problem:
- Joseph Smith claimed to have translated ancient Egyptian papyri
- The papyri were discovered and preserved
- Modern Egyptologists have definitively established these papyri have nothing to do with Abraham
- The translated content reflects 19th-century Joseph Smith theology, not ancient Egyptian texts

This represents provable, falsifiable translation error.

### Joseph Smith's Polygamy

The letter documents Joseph Smith's polygamy and the LDS Church's changing narrative:
- Joseph Smith practiced polygamy while publicly denying it
- He married women as young as 14
- He married women already married to other men
- Early church leaders and members were misled about the practice
- The church officially discontinued polygamy only after government pressure, not revelation

The section details how the church's official narrative changed over time as evidence accumulated.

### The First Vision Accounts

Joseph Smith gave multiple, contradicting accounts of the First Vision (when he claimed to have seen God and Jesus):
- The 1832 account (Joseph's own handwriting) differs significantly from the official 1838 account
- Different accounts describe different beings appearing (God alone vs. God and Jesus)
- The accounts emphasize different messages and purposes
- The official account became standardized only decades after the alleged event

This raises questions about which account is accurate and why the story changed.

### Blacks and the Priesthood

Until 1978, the LDS Church banned Black men from receiving the priesthood—a unique position among mainstream Christian denominations:
- The church taught this ban was divinely mandated
- No scriptural basis for the ban exists
- Joseph Smith actually ordained at least one Black man to the priesthood
- The church changed its position only after intense social pressure and litigation threats

The church's explanation shifted from claiming this was God's will to claiming it was an early leadership mistake.

### DNA Evidence and Native American Ancestry

The Book of Mormon describes Lehi's family traveling from Jerusalem to the Americas around 600 BCE and populating the Americas. Modern DNA analysis shows:
- Native Americans descended from Asian populations crossing the Bering Strait
- No genetic evidence supports Middle Eastern ancestry in Native American populations
- The church previously taught that Native Americans were descended from the Book of Mormon peoples
- Modern genetic evidence contradicts this foundational Book of Mormon claim

## Why the CES Letter is Significant

### Comprehensive and Detailed

The letter doesn't make general criticisms; it documents specific historical issues with citations and evidence. Readers can verify claims themselves.

### Sincere Tone

Rather than being antagonistic or mocking, the letter reads as a sincere plea from a devoted believer asking for answers. This tone made it accessible to believing members who might dismiss more hostile critiques.

### Addressed to Leadership

By framing questions to church educational leadership, Runnells positioned it as internal dialogue rather than external attack, making it less dismissible for members.

### Systematically Devastating

The cumulative effect of addressing multiple problems (translation issues, historical inaccuracies, doctrinal problems, scientific contradictions) is more powerful than individual critiques. It demonstrates a pattern of problems, not isolated issues.

### Triggered Institutional Response

The church recognized the document's impact and created a formal response (the "Gospel Topics Essays"), validating its significance and inadvertently confirming many of its points.

## How the Church Responded

The LDS Church officially published "Gospel Topics Essays" addressing many CES Letter issues:
- Essays on the Book of Abraham, DNA evidence, blacks and the priesthood, and other topics
- The essays generally acknowledged problems but framed them differently than Runnells
- Essays sometimes confirmed CES Letter points while attempting alternative explanations

The church's response validated the CES Letter's legitimacy and demonstrated that these issues were serious enough to require official address.

## Accessing the CES Letter

The complete CES Letter is freely available at [www.cesletter.org](https://www.cesletter.org/).

The website includes:
- The full letter in multiple formats
- Supporting documentation and sources
- FAQ addressing common responses
- Translations in multiple languages

The letter is also available in printed book format for those preferring physical copies.

## Impact on Faith Transitions

The CES Letter is cited repeatedly by former members as crucial to their deconstruction:
- Many credit it with first exposing them to substantive historical problems
- Its comprehensive nature prevented dismissal as isolated criticism
- Its sincere tone made it accessible to believing members
- Multiple faith transitions specifically mention the CES Letter as a turning point

Online communities for former Mormons frequently reference it, and testimonies consistently include its role in deconstruction.

## Limitations and Critiques

While influential, the CES Letter has limitations:
- It focuses on historical and doctrinal issues rather than psychology of belief or control mechanisms
- It doesn't address the emotional and relationship aspects of leaving
- Some critiques are more compelling than others
- It's best used alongside other resources exploring psychological, emotional, and relationship dimensions

## The CES Letter in Context

The CES Letter represents a broader phenomenon: the internet democratizing access to critical information about religions. Before widespread internet access, members had limited ways to encounter sustained, documented critiques. The CES Letter exemplifies how individuals can now compile and share comprehensive challenges to religious claims.

It's not the only critical resource about Mormonism, but it's arguably the most influential single document.

## Final Thoughts

The CES Letter represents a watershed moment in Mormon deconstruction. It demonstrated that substantive, documented critiques could trigger large-scale faith transitions. It also demonstrated that the internet had fundamentally changed how religious organizations could maintain information control.

For Mormons considering faith transitions, the CES Letter provides comprehensive documentation of historical and doctrinal problems. For researchers of religious change, it exemplifies how information access affects belief systems.

---

**Note**: The existence and influence of the CES Letter doesn't prove Mormonism false any more than church essays prove it true. The value of the letter is in presenting documented historical information that individuals can evaluate for themselves—the opposite of the information control that characterized pre-internet Mormonism.

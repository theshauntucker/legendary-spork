# Is Mormonism a Cult? A BITE Model Analysis

The question of whether The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS Church) is a cult is complex and often polarizing. Rather than attempting a binary yes/no answer, this analysis applies Dr. Steven Hassan's BITE Model to examine specific mechanisms of control within Mormonism and explore where it falls on a spectrum of organizational authoritarianism.

## Understanding the BITE Model Context

It's important to note that the BITE Model is not a diagnostic tool for cult membership but rather a framework for identifying potentially harmful influence and control mechanisms. Organizations don't exist in binary categories—they exist on a spectrum. Many mainstream religions share some BITE characteristics without being universally classified as destructive cults.

## Behavior Control in Mormonism

### Dietary Restrictions and Dress Codes

The LDS Word of Wisdom prohibits alcohol, tobacco, coffee, and tea. While presented as health guidance, adherence becomes a marker of faithfulness and temple worthiness. Members who violate these standards face restrictions in temple access and potential social consequences.

The temple garment requirement (special underclothing worn by observant members) represents another behavioral control mechanism. Members are instructed to wear these garments at all times, creating constant physical reinforcement of religious identity and increasing social visibility of religious commitment.

### Time and Social Control

Mormonism demands substantial time investment:
- Weekly 3-hour sacrament meetings
- Multiple meetings for various programs
- Mission service expectations (2 years for men, 18 months for women)
- Temple attendance requirements for temple-recommended members
- Frequent home and visiting teaching assignments

This time commitment reduces exposure to outside perspectives and strengthens in-group bonding while making exit practically difficult, especially for those with families and social networks entirely within the church.

### Finance and Tithing

Members are expected to pay 10% of gross income as tithing, with full financial transparency to church leaders (bishops and stake presidents). Temple recommend status—required for many religious privileges—depends partly on tithing payment verification. Members tithing less than expected may be questioned about their faithfulness.

## Information Control in Mormonism

### Restricted Historical Narratives

The LDS Church has historically controlled which historical documents members encounter. The "CES Letter," written by Jeremy Runnells, catalogs historical issues that the church discouraged public discussion of, including:
- Joseph Smith's involvement in folk magic and treasure digging before founding the church
- The Book of Abraham's discrepancies with Egyptology
- Polygamy's historical extent and details
- Early racial restrictions on Black membership
- Discrepancies between historical accounts of First Vision and official narrative

While the church has recently published more historical information through "Gospel Topics Essays," these often frame problematic histories as misunderstandings rather than directly addressing incongruities.

### Media and Narrative Control

Official church media through Deseret News emphasize faith-promoting narratives. Independent Mormon media (blogs, podcasts, YouTube channels) that question church narratives face pressure, with some creators experiencing social ostracism or employment difficulties.

## Thought Control in Mormonism

### Loaded Language and Terminology

Mormonism uses distinctive language that reinforces group boundaries:
- "True Church" (only Mormonism)
- "Apostasy" (leaving the church)
- "Anti-Mormon" (any criticism)
- "The Spirit" (internal validation)
- "Testimony" (emotional conviction treated as factual verification)

This language discourages critical examination by framing doubt as spiritual weakness rather than legitimate inquiry.

### Pressure Against Critical Thinking

Members are taught to gain testimony through "feeling" the Holy Ghost rather than through evidence and logical analysis. Questioning is reframed as lack of faith. Comments in meetings emphasizing critical analysis are sometimes met with gentle correction toward faith-based acceptance.

## Emotional Control in Mormonism

### Shame and Guilt Mechanisms

The LDS Church uses shame as a behavioral motivator:
- Sexual purity standards create shame around normal adolescent development
- Members who leave are often characterized as "lost" or "deceived"
- Public transgression results in public humiliation through church discipline
- Guilt about inadequate religious practice is continuously reinforced

### Isolation and Family Pressure

Members who leave or question the church face serious social consequences:
- Family relationships become strained or severed
- Friend groups dissolve (most friends are church members)
- Shunning is informal but effective, particularly in heavily Mormon communities

## Where Does Mormonism Fall on the Spectrum?

Based on this BITE analysis, Mormonism demonstrates moderate to high control across all four categories, placing it in the upper-moderate range of the spectrum—demonstrating characteristics associated with harmful high-control groups.
